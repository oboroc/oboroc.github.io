Introduction

MSX stands for MicroSoft eXtented architecture. MSX and MSX-2 computers was built around 8-bit 3.5MHz Zilog Z-80 CPU and special graphics and sound co-processors. MSX standard (later known as MSX-1) was introduced in 1982 by Microsoft and ASCII Corporation of Japan. MSX developers had a very ambicious plans: to make MSX a world standard for home computers, like VHS for home video is. And this plan was partially realized: MSX became probably the most popular school and home computer in Brazil, Japan, USSR and many other countries for many years (from 1984 until 1989). Computers, compatible with MSX and MSX-2 specs, was produced by almost all major Japanese, Korejan and even by some european brands (Sony, Phillips, Panasonic, Yamaha, Toshiba, Daewoo to name a few). The next generation MSX-2 standard was introduced in 1985, and it was incredibly innovative for that time: 16/256 color videomodes, big and full-color hardware accelerated sprites, Yamaha OPL synthesizer, 720Kb 1.44" floppy disk drive... Starting with 1988 MSX became a little bit outdated, because new cheap and powerful 16-bit computers filled the market: Amigas, AtariTT/ST, ACORN Archimedes, SHARP X68000 and, ofcouse, IBM PCs and PS/2. But in 1990 ASCII tried to get customers attention back by releasing MSX-2+ specs, which had several big benefits in comparition to MSX-2. The last MSX standard was MSX-TurboR. Those computers had many significant changes in hardware, Z-80 was used mainly for compatibility with old software, while native programs used R800 RISC CPU. Both MSX-2+ and MSX-TurboR machines had built-in Kanji support and was distributed only in Japan and Koreja. You can find more information about this great platform in The Ultimate MSX FAQ.

In September 1990 I saw MSX computer for the first time. It was Yamaha MSX-2 KUVT. I wrote on this computer my first program in Turbo Pascal 3.0 for CP/M-80. And I played on it my first computer games. My most favorite game of all times is still Konami's Metal Gear. But Vampire Killer, ZanacEx and Konami's Knightmare are also extremly nice games.

MSX emulation

Today it is very difficult to find a working MSX machine. But, thanks to hard work of emulators authors, we can run MSX applications and games on PC. The best MSX emulators in my opinion are fMSX and RuMSX. There are several other good emulators, but I prefer to use fMSX and RuMSX.
